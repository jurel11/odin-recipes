Lechon Baboy

Description

Lechon, derived from a Spanish word for roasted suckling pig is one of the most popular dishes in the Philippines. The slowly-roasted suckling pig is usually stuffed with lemongrass, tamarind, garlic, onions, and chives, and is then roasted on a large bamboo spit over an open fire.

It is traditionally served whole on a platter, at celebrations and festive events such as weddings and Christmas. Once the meat is properly roasted and falls off the bone, people tend to eat every part of the pig, and the crispy, reddish-brown, crackling skin is especially beloved.

Ingredients

  • Pork
  • Onion
  • Lemongrass
  • Garlic
  • Bay Leaves
  • Black Pepper

Steps

  1. Prepare the Pig: Clean the pig thoroughly, removing the entrails and washing it inside and out with boiling water to remove any hair. Drain and pat dry
  2. Season the Pig: Rub the inside and outside of the pig with salt and black pepper. Stuff the pig with lemongrass, garlic, onions, and bay leaves.
  3. Skewer the Pig: Skewer the pig on a long bamboo pole or metal rod, ensuring it is securely fastened.
  4. Roast the Pig: Prepare a charcoal pit or large grill. The pig should be roasted over medium heat. Roast the pig slowly, turning it regularly to ensure even cooking. This process can take 4-5 hours depending on the size of the pig. Baste the pig with a mixture of oil and soy sauce every 30 minutes to keep the skin crispy and flavorful.
  5. Check for Doneness: The pig is done when the skin is golden brown and crispy, and the meat is tender and juicy. You can check the internal temperature with a meat thermometer; it should read at least 160°F (71°C).
  6. Serve: Carefully remove the pig from the skewer and let it rest for a few minutes. Carve and serve with traditional Filipino dipping sauces like liver sauce or vinegar with garlic and chili.
